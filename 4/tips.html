Centrosymmetry — Vowels and Semi-vowels

Unlike consonant letters, new vowel letters are created by putting dots on semi-vovel letters, as the chart below shows. You can also comprehend this as putting dots on the “centrosymmertry” bases, which is assigned to semi-vovels.

Grrrols — When “r” is a Vowel

Pronouncing ẓ⟨eo⟩ can be a little tricky: we can say that it’s a “vowelized” English ⟨r⟩ like in “brrr” and “grrr”. but techinally it’s a [ɨ], more like Russian ⟨ы⟩. And after a q⟨s⟩ or b⟨sh⟩, it tend to be an apical vowel like the ⟨i⟩ after ⟨s⟩ or ⟨sh⟩ in Mandarin.

Last, and luckily the least, when not stressed, ẓ⟨eo⟩ can be simply pronounced as a “schwa” /ə/.

How will This End?

Like a handful of languages in our universe, Patchélogue verbs’ endings change depending on who its subject is. For example, you say “Sṣ żıqɒı̣”(I love) but “Dṡ żıqɒı̣qɑ”

, and there are many other endings you will learn later.

Eiki, Keiki and Cake-y

In English, you write a proper noun in sentence case. But in German and Patchélogue, you write every noun in sentence case. But there are more unique capitalizing rules in Patchélogue:

  1. A pronoun should be written in sentence case, too.
  2. You don’t write a sentence in sentence case. Yeah, seriously. The author found capital letters in Patchélogue too many, that’s why.
  3. The last letter of a proper noun should be in upper case, too.

In Patchélogue, both the first and the last letter of a proper noun should be capitalized, like I̱ıᴚĪ⟨EykI⟩ and ꓘı̣ıᴚĪ⟨KeykI⟩. And then there’s an everyday noun ꓘı̣ıᴚi⟨Keyki⟩ that means “cake”:
