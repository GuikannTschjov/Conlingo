When “r” is a Vowel — Apical Vowel

Pronouncing ẓ⟨eo⟩ can be a little tricky: we can say that it’s a “vowelized” English ⟨r⟩, but techinally it’s a [ɨ], more like Russian ⟨ы⟩. And after a q⟨s⟩ or b⟨sh⟩, it tend to be an apical vowel like the ⟨i⟩ after ⟨s⟩ or ⟨sh⟩ in Mandarin.

Last, and luckily the least, when not stressed, ẓ⟨eo⟩ can be simply pronounced as a “schwa” /ə/.

Eyki, Keyki and Cake-y

In English, you write a proper noun in sentence case. But in German and Patchélogue, you write any noun in sentence case. The capitalizing rules of Patchélogue is different from German by three aspect:

  1. A pronoun should be written in sentence case, too.
  2. You don’t write a sentence in sentence case. Yeah, seriously. The author found capital letters in Patchélogue too many, that’s why.
  3. The last letter of a proper noun should be in upper case, too.

In Patchélogue, both the first and the last letter of a proper noun should be capitalized, like I̱ıᴚĪ⟨EykI⟩ and ꓘı̣ıᴚĪ⟨KeykI⟩. Those are, respectively, names of goddess of justice, and goddess of creativeness in Patchélogue spakers’ religion. And then there’s an everyday noun ꓘı̣ıᴚi⟨Keyki⟩ that means “cake”:
