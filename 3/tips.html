Gender but it’s about Consciousness

There are 2 grammatical genders in Patchélogue, which is not divided by sеx but by consciousness.

Unlike Spanish or German, a noun’s grammatical gender isn’t fixed: a vehicle can be “conscious” when operated by a driver, a person can be “unconscious” when sleeping hard. That means you don’t memorize things like la mesa or der Tisch in Patchélogue.

Many words, most of which you haven’t learnt, changes accroading to the noun’s gender.

Du et le bon Gotis. ⟨Du et le bon Gotis.⟩
“You and the good fishes(unconscious).”

A Semicolon; a Question Mark? An Exclamation Mark!

You may have noticed sentences from last lesson ends in a “comma”, and a subscript 9 — or an empty comma — appearing where you’d expect a comma. Yes, those are periods and commas in Patchélogue!

Just like Greek and Ethiopian, Patchélogue has a different set of punctuations. While Greek use “;” for question marks, Patchélogue use it for exclamation marks! There’s even a “exclamation comma” for sentences like “Hey, you!”, which looked like a empty semicolon: “⨟”.
