Turning and Streching — Four of a Kind

You’ve seen how the letters “ʎ⟨m⟩”, “y⟨f⟩”, and “ʌ⟨b⟩” resemble each other, but have you met “v⟨p⟩”? Now getting all the pieces together, they surely look related, doesn’t they?

Unturned, unstreched:
Vv⟨p⟩		 Turned, unstreched:
Λʌ⟨b⟩
Unturned, streched:
Yy⟨f⟩		 Turned, streched:
⅄ʎ⟨m⟩

That’s the two ways new Higilaya letters were created after the barbarians found the comic books. In the following lesson, you will learn two other “unturned-unstreched” letters ꓘᴚ⟨k⟩ and Πn⟨l⟩. Can you figure out what their “relatives” may look like?

Turn around!

In Greek, the letter “sigma” comes in two forms: one that you’ve seen on your physics class, and one word-final form “ς”. Patchélogue does the same thing with Πn⟨l⟩ as well: in the end of a word, the letter turns upside-down and makes a “dark-L” sound!

Unlike Greek sigma, in Higilaya alphabet, Πn⟨l⟩ and Цu⟨lh⟩ are considered different letters.
